Marijuana Law Reform in the US

I was active in drug policy reform activism for several years. This included working as an intern for the Marijuana Policy Project (MPP), working in various capacities for Law Enforcement Against Prohibition and writing articles for a similar nonprofit advocacy organization, Students for Sensible Drug Policy. The diagram above, from MPP, depicts the current state of state-level marijuana laws, while pending changes to federal law can be found here. Those in black have both removed criminal penalties for possession of small amounts of marijuana and passed medical marijuana laws. Those in dark gray have only removed criminal penalties for possession of small amounts, which is commonly known as decriminalization. Those in light gray have passed medical marijuana measures only, while those in blue have legalized marijuana in a similar manner to alcohol, with a minimum legal age of 21. The data in the table below consist of the same information, but in a responsive table format which is implemented with jQuery. The presence of a decriminalization measure in a legalized state is of course superfluous, but it is noted as it reflects the fact that there were earlier reforms to the law before the recent legalization.

Currently, about 5.3% of the US population about 16.9 million out of and estimated 319 million people, live in the four states where marijuana legalization measures have been passed. The majority of Americans live in one of the 27 states (or the District of Columbia) where some sort of marijuana law reform has been passed. Fourteen states have decriminalization in effect, while 23 states plus the District of Columbia (including many of the same states with decriminalization) have medical marijuana laws in effect. These numbers are a significant increase from the state of affairs as recently as 2013.

*Whether Missouri qualifies as a state with marijuana decriminalization is debatable. A bill was passed in May of 2014 which removes the possibility of jail time for possession of up to 10 grams of marijuana. However, the law will not take effect until 2017, only applies to first offenses, and would not actually remove the threat of criminal penalties other than incarceration.
*Washington, DC passed a referendum on November 4th of 2014 legalizing the possession (up to a certain amount) and home cultivation of marijuana, and although House Republicans threatened to overturn the measure, there was no action by Congress within 30 days, so it went into effect on February 26th of 2015.
*New York passed a decriminalization measure in 1973 but there have since then been complaints of frequent arrests for minor marijuana possession, arguably due to exploitation of a loophole in the law.
